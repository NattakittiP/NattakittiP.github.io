About

I am a Thai student and early-career researcher currently studying in the International Bachelor Program in Informatics (IBPI) at Yuan Ze University, Taiwan. My work focuses on the intersection of machine learning, data science, and health systems, with a particular interest in building reliable and transparent AI methods for real-world decision making.

My academic journey began in Artificial Intelligence Engineering at Satit Prasarnmit Demonstration School, where I developed a Thai-language chatbot using natural language processing (NLP) and competed in robotics competitions, placing 5th nationally in the World Robot Olympiad (WRO). These early experiences shaped my interest in how intelligent systems can be designed, evaluated, and applied to complex real-world problems.

I later spent two years studying in the College of Medicine at Rangsit University. While this experience deepened my understanding of human health and biomedical systems, it also helped me realize that my strongest motivation lies in analyzing complex problems through data, algorithms, and computational modeling. This realization led me to transition fully into informatics and artificial intelligence.

My current research explores topics such as machine learning evaluation and validation, reproducibility in AI research, and the design of leakage-controlled machine learning pipelines. I am particularly interested in understanding how small methodological choices, such as data splitting strategies, preprocessing protocols, or calibration procedures, can significantly influence model performance and scientific conclusions.

Beyond technical performance, I care deeply about the trustworthiness of AI. My work aims to develop evaluation frameworks that emphasize transparency, robustness, and realistic reporting, especially in domains related to health data, biomedical informatics, and policy-relevant analytics.

In the long term, I hope to contribute to the development of reliable and responsible AI systems that can support evidence-based decisions in healthcare, science, and public policy.

Whole Blood Viscosity and Triglyceride Clearance: An Association Analysis Relevant to Cancer Risk (2025)

Accepted after triple-blind peer review and selected for publication and oral presentation at IEEE ICBCB 2026
(EI Compendex & Scopus Proceedings)
2026 14th International Conference on Bioinformatics and Computational Biology
Supervised by Dr. Qazi Mazhar ul Haq (Assistant Professor, Department of Computer Science and Engineering, Yuan Ze University)

This study proposes a data-driven risk stratification framework using whole blood viscosity (WBV) and triglyceride clearance rate (TCR) as potential metabolic–oncologic markers. Machine Learning–Based Modeling of Postprandial Triglyceride Response (2025)

This project develops a leakage-controlled and explainable machine learning framework for predicting elevated postprandial triglyceride (TG) response using only routine fasting biomarkers. A Multi-World Synthetic Benchmark for Evaluating RSCE in Clinical Machine Learning (2025)

The journal manuscript has been published in IEEE Access (SCIE, Q1)
Supervised by Dr. Qazi Mazhar ul Haq (Assistant Professor, Department of Computer Science and Engineering, Yuan Ze University) and Dr. Yi-Jheng Huang (Associate Professor, Director of International Bachelor Program in Informatics, Yuan Ze University)

This work proposes a seven-world synthetic benchmarking framework for systematically evaluating model integrity in clinical machine learning across four complementary dimensions: Reliability, Robustness, Calibration Stability, and Explainability Stability (RSCE). Each synthetic world introduces analytically controlled perturbations, including distribution shift, missingness, measurement noise, surrogate-feature corruption, nonlinear distortion, and label noise.

The benchmark evaluates a diverse 20-model clinical ML zoo using a unified RSCE scoring scheme, SHAP-based attribution stability analysis, calibration-drift metrics, and bootstrap uncertainty quantification. Results show that tree-based ensemble models consistently achieve the most balanced integrity profiles, while several neural and kernel-based models exhibit increased sensitivity to missingness, proxy drift, and nonlinear perturbations.

By providing a fully reproducible, leakage-aware, and analytically transparent evaluation pipeline, this study offers a rigorous framework for pre-deployment stress testing of clinical ML systems, supporting integrity-aware model selection and governance under deployment-time uncertainty.

A Leakage-Controlled Machine Learning Framework for Postprandial Triglyceride Phenotyping Using Synthetic Clinical Data (2025)

The Journal manuscript has been submitted to Scientific Reports (Springer Nature, Q1) and is presently undergoing peer review
Supervised by Dr. Qazi Mazhar ul Haq (Assistant Professor, Department of Computer Science and Engineering, Yuan Ze University) and Dr. Yi-Jheng Huang (Associate Professor, Director of International Bachelor Program in Informatics, Yuan Ze University)

This study introduces a strict leakage-controlled and reproducible machine learning framework to examine whether whole-blood viscosity (WBV) independently influences postprandial triglyceride clearance rate (TCR). Using a statistically reconstructed synthetic clinical cohort, the pipeline integrates fold-specific preprocessing, nested cross-validation, probability calibration, bootstrap robustness testing, and SHAP-based interpretability.

Across all models and sensitivity analyses, the framework reveals a consistent null association between WBV and short-term triglyceride clearance after accounting for key metabolic factors. The work demonstrates the scientific importance of transparent null findings, showcasing how rigorous leakage prevention and open synthetic-data pipelines can improve methodological reliability in biomedical informatics and strengthen future clinical decision-support research.

Dataset Edition as a Source of Evaluation Divergence in Clinical Machine Learning: A Controlled Study on MIMIC-IV Demo and Full Releases (2026)

Journal manuscript has been submitted to BMC Medical Research Methodology (Springer Nature, Q1)
Supervised by Dr. Qazi Mazhar ul Haq (Assistant Professor, Department of Computer Science and Engineering, Yuan Ze University) and Dr. Yi-Jheng Huang (Associate Professor, Director of International Bachelor Program in Informatics, Yuan Ze University)

This work examines dataset edition as a structural factor in clinical machine learning evaluation, demonstrating that the widely used MIMIC-IV demo dataset cannot be treated as a simple small-sample proxy of the full cohort. Under a strictly controlled experimental design, the study isolates the effect of dataset choice by holding preprocessing, modeling, hyperparameters, and evaluation protocols fixed.

Across a diverse set of models, performance metrics, robustness scenarios, and interpretability analyses, the study reveals systematic divergence between demo- and full-based conclusions. These divergences extend beyond pointwise performance to affect model ranking, calibration behavior, robustness profiles, and explanation stability, even when identical pipelines are used.

Importantly, the work introduces decision-level evaluation via prevalence-standardized positive predictive value, showing that operating-point conclusions can differ markedly between dataset editions even after explicitly controlling for outcome prevalence. This finding highlights that dataset-induced divergence reflects differences in error structure and threshold behavior rather than base-rate effects alone.

By framing dataset choice as a regime-defining component of the evaluation pipeline, this study provides a principled explanation for inconsistent findings across clinical ML benchmarks and emphasizes the need for transparent reporting, stability analysis, and dataset-aware interpretation when drawing comparative or deployment-oriented conclusions.

Clinical Machine Learning · Dataset Shift · Evaluation Stability · Decision-Level Metrics · Reproducibility

Protocol-Level Auditing of Machine Learning Evaluation: Disentangling Split Policy, Leakage, and Decision Stability (2026)

Conference manuscript has been submitted to The 23rd The International Joint Conference on Computer Science and Software Engineering (JCSSE 2026) and is presently undergoing peer review
Supervised by Dr. Qazi Mazhar ul Haq (Assistant Professor, Department of Computer Science and Engineering, Yuan Ze University)

This work argues that seed-based reproducibility is not equivalent to protocol validity: stable cross-validation metrics across random seeds can still occur under flawed or poorly specified evaluation designs. To address this, the study proposes a protocol-level audit framework that systematically separates three often-confounded factors in empirical ML studies: split policy, leakage protocol, and model family.

The paper constructs an explicit audit matrix spanning two outer split strategies (StratifiedKFold vs. GroupKFold), four leakage variants (P0–P3, from strictly fold-safe nested CV with calibration-before-tuning to progressively violated settings), and five model families (L2-Logistic Regression, Linear SVM, Random Forest, XGBoost, ExtraTrees). Model selection is performed via a predefined lexicographic rule (AUROC → AP → Brier) to avoid metric cherry-picking.

To align evaluation with real decision-making, the study introduces winner flip percentage as a decision-level stability metric, quantifying how often the selected winner model changes under seed perturbations (20 independent seeds). Across 260 configurations and 1,300 outer-fold evaluations, the winner identity remains invariant (XGBoost) across split policies, leakage variants, and seed sweeps, while still revealing that split policy induces measurable performance shifts and that leakage effects are configuration-dependent (not uniformly inflationary).

A synthetic control experiment further validates pipeline coherence: injecting 15% MCAR missingness yields a clear degradation signal whose magnitude exceeds protocol-induced shifts, reinforcing that data-quality effects can dominate protocol choices even when the selected winner remains unchanged.

Protocol-Level Auditing for Reliable Clinical Machine Learning Evaluation (2026)

The Journal manuscript has been submitted to IEEE Journal of Biomedical and Health Informatics (IEEE, Q1) and is presently undergoing peer review
Supervised by Dr. Qazi Mazhar ul Haq (Assistant Professor, Department of Computer Science and Engineering, Yuan Ze University) and Dr. Yi-Jheng Huang (Associate Professor, Director of International Bachelor Program in Informatics, Yuan Ze University)

This work introduces a protocol-level audit framework for assessing the reliability of model evaluation and model selection in clinical machine learning. Rather than proposing a new predictor, the study examines how seemingly minor evaluation decisions, such as data splitting policy, preprocessing placement, and the ordering of hyperparameter tuning and probability calibration, can systematically change reported performance and even alter the identity of the selected “best” model.

Evaluation reliability is operationalized through a locked audit matrix of leakage-explicit protocol variants (P0–P3), contrasting a strict fold-safe pipeline with controlled protocol deviations that emulate common methodological errors. The framework is evaluated across three complementary cohorts, a real ICU mortality cohort from MIMIC-IV, a synthetic physiologic dataset for controlled stress testing, and an external multi-centre ICU cohort from eICU with leakage-guarded feature hygiene. Five representative model families are assessed using nested cross-validation, calibration-aware training, and deterministic experiment logging.

Beyond conventional performance metrics, the study treats ranking stability as a primary evaluation objective, introducing diagnostics such as winner flip percentage, rank-correlation analysis, and top-2 margin inspection, along with stress tests under missingness and label-prevalence shift. Results show that model rankings and winner identity can change under protocol variation and realistic data perturbations, with many reversals arising in near-tie performance regimes. By reframing evaluation as an auditable, stress-testable process, the work provides a reproducible and leakage-aware framework for more trustworthy comparative evaluation in biomedical machine learning.

Machine Learning Identifies Visibility as a Dominant Perceptual Predictor of Human Text Legibility (2026)

The conference manuscript has been submitted to The 2nd International Conference on Artificial Intelligence and Education
(ICAIE 2026) and is presently undergoing peer review
Supervised by Dr. Qazi Mazhar ul Haq (Assistant Professor, Department of Computer Science and Engineering, Yuan Ze University)

This project introduces a human–machine learning framework for predicting whether a student can visually read another student’s written answers under realistic classroom viewing conditions. A 300-scenario multimodal dataset was constructed, capturing geometric factors (distance, angular size, text height), display characteristics, head-pose angles, and a continuous visibility score derived from human perception.

Using this dataset, Logistic Regression and Random Forest classifiers were trained to predict the binary outcome can_read, achieving a test-set AUC of 0.962. Feature-importance analyses show that the visibility score acts as a highly informative perceptual variable, dominating all geometric predictors and effectively serving as a compact legibility descriptor. Partial-dependence curves further reveal clear perceptual thresholds and the dominant influence of visibility over raw geometry.

This work provides the first visibility-based framework for assessing cheating feasibility in physical examination settings. The findings support the design of visibility-aware AI proctoring systems, classroom layout optimisation tools, and fairer technology-enhanced assessment environments based on interpretable human-vision principles.

Visibility as a Perceptual Mediator for Human Text Legibility: An Interpretable Machine Learning Study (2025)

Journal manuscript under preparation for submission to IEEE Access (SCIE, Q1)
An extended and substantially enhanced version of the ICAIE 2026 conference paper
Supervised by Dr. Qazi Mazhar ul Haq (Assistant Professor, Department of Computer Science and Engineering, Yuan Ze University)

This work investigates text legibility as a perceptual feasibility problem rather than a behavioral inference task, introducing visibility as a human-rated perceptual mediator between geometric viewing conditions and actual reading success. Through interpretable machine learning, the study examines whether visibility constitutes a perceptually sufficient statistic for predicting human text legibility.

Using a controlled dataset of classroom-style scenarios with systematic variations in viewing distance, angular character size, head pose, display medium, and contrast, the study conducts extensive ablation, calibration, and robustness analyses. Results show that models trained solely on visibility achieve near-ceiling predictive performance, while geometric and contextual features provide marginal and inconsistent gains once visibility is observed.

Compared to the ICAIE 2026 version, this IEEE Access manuscript introduces expanded experimental protocols, including large-scale repeated cross-validation, structured setting-shift stress tests, probability calibration analysis, and feature-level robustness diagnostics. These extensions provide a more rigorous and transparent examination of model sufficiency, interpretability, and generalization under perceptual uncertainty.

By explicitly separating perceptual feasibility from behavioral intent, this work offers a principled foundation for designing fair, interpretable, and deployment-aware AI systems in educational assessment and human-centered sensing environments.

Ranking Instability Under Latent Evaluation Shifts: Integrity-Aware Model Selection Beyond Leaderboards (2026)

The conference manuscript is under preparation for submission to the Fortieth Annual Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NeurIPS 2026)

This work studies comparative evaluation failure in model selection: even when multiple models achieve near-ceiling discrimination in one plausible evaluation environment, their relative ordering (rankings) can systematically reverse under subtle, under-specified changes such as dataset editions, labeling policies, cohort rules, and split protocols—termed latent evaluation shifts.

Using controlled multi-environment evaluations, the study demonstrates that near-perfect AUROC (≈ 0.998) on the MIMIC-IV full release can coexist with large ranking divergence when switching to the official MIMIC-IV demo release (AUROC ≈ 0.60–0.75), with ~60% of model pairs reversing their ordering. Extensive audits (e.g., leakage hardening, split checks, and sensitivity analyses) indicate the effect is structural rather than random noise.

The work introduces STABLE-MERGE, an integrity-aware selection framework that:
(1) formalizes ranking instability via pairwise preference flips, global flip rate, and model-level flip involvement (FlipInv)
(2) connects ranking fragility to deployment consequences via Winner’s Regret under environment transfer
(3) performs selection by enforcing joint constraints on calibration error (ECE) and ranking stability (FlipInv).

Crucially, STABLE-MERGE can explicitly certify infeasibility when no candidate satisfies minimum integrity requirements (calibration + stability), and then returns a flagged minimax-regret fallback to reduce worst-case deployment loss under environment uncertainty, making fragility transparent rather than forcing a potentially non-transferable “leaderboard winner.”
